Biogeochemical Responses to a Pulsing Hydroperiod in a Vermicast Soil Amendment System


“Much of the planet’s biodiversity resides in the soil: it’s estimated that an acre of soil may contain 900 pounds of earthworms, 2400 pounds of fungi, 1500 pounds of bacteria, 133 pounds of protozoa, 890 pounds of arthropods. One gram of soil may hold one billion bacteria, of which only 5 percent have been discovered.” (Cho et al., 2012). Determining the nature of the relationships between these complex factors is a daunting ordeal. improvements of soils by  introducing worm tea as a soil amendment and liquid fertilizer, sets into motion significant changes in the biogeochemistry of the soil. These aforementioned changes affect the suitability of the soil for different species of vegetation, macrofauna, mesofauna, microbial and fungal life, and set the context for the long-term development of the soil. In turn, changes in vegetation,and present fauna can substantially modify the biogeochemistry of the soil. 
This article reviews literature and discusses data collected on biogeochemical responses to use of a vermicast based liquid soil amendment with a pulsing hydroperiod to mimic tidal flushing. It draws primarily on cases from the United States but also includes several important studies that were carried out in southeast Asia and in Europe. 


Defining the System
One of the difficulties in studying the effectiveness of soil amendments is the uniqueness of each vermi-system. Differences in nutrient input, hydroperiod, fill/sediment composition, frequency of organic matter deposition, and other factors all lead to significant biogeochemical differences among vermi-systems. Responses to amendments produced would be expected to differ as well. The details of soil restoration and rehabilitation history due to vermicast systems are often unknown or not reported in literature. Even when they are, it can be hard to know which of the details are important. 
To address this variability, the following is a rough classification of vermicompost system types described by Ronda Sherman in the Worm Farmers Handbook
Windrows
Windrows are linear piles containing bedding and feedstocks that will eventually reach heights of 24-30 inches (61-76 cm).They are typically 4 to 8 feet (1.2-2.4 m) wide and however long the space allows. Many people living in milder climates, such as the west coast of the United States, have established outdoor windrows for vermicast production, though windrows can also be set up inside large buildings. 
A front-end loader, skid steer, shovel, or pitchfork can be used to make a windrow. Simply spread t 6-to 8- inch deep (15-20 cm) layer of bedding of the desired width and length on bare ground and add 1 pound (0.5 kg)of Eisenia fetida earthworms per square foot (0.09 m^2) of surface area. Its important to smother vegetation first so grass and weeds don't grow into the windrow, which would interfere with harvest. Since composting earthworms do not burrow, you don't have to worry about them migrating out of the windrow into the soil. After the worms have settled into the bedding, apply feedstock in a layer 1 to 1 ½ inches (2.5-3.8 cm) deep, and wait until the feed has been consumed before adding more. During cold weather, 3 to 4 inches (7.6 - 10.2 cm) of feedstock may be applied to the top of the windrow to help keep the worms warm. Although many worm growers have success with outdoor windrows, some folks report problems with excess moisture, anaerobic areas, and predators such as moles and birds. Vermicomposting in windrows is a slow process that can take up to 12 months to produce finished vermicast. 
Pits or Trenches
Pits or trenches have been used for decades. As with other vermicomposting systems, you lay down moist bedding, add worms, and apply thin layers of the feedstock on top. People like pits for vermicomposting because they are simple holes in the ground - No construction required. The earthen sides provide insulation against both hot and cold weather. Some people choose to line their pits with cement blocks. However, raising earthworms in pits can strain your back from bending over often. Another disadvantage is that pits can be flooded by heavy rains. 
A successful trench model was established in North Carolina in the early 2000s. A hog manure vermicomposter named Tom Christianberry was having trouble with his continuous flow-through bins overheating, so he created a trench system that allowed the ground to help moderate excessively col or hot temperatures. Inside a pole barn he dug five trenches 4 feet wide by 200 feet long by 21 inches deep (1.2 x 61.0 x 0.5 m). Between the trenches were hard packed dirt and gravel lanes that could support a tractor. To feed the worms, he drove a tractor straddling the top of a trench. The tractor pulled a manure spreader that was calibrated to deposit hog manure into the trench, and a worker would then use a rake to evenly spread the manure 1 inch (2.5 cm) deep across it. He carried out this feeding process weekly using raw hop manure that had been flushed out of a barn and run through a solids separator. 
Before you commit to setting up a pit system, ask yourself whether you will be able to handle all of the bending that it will require to apply feedstock, check the worms and bed conditions, and harvest the vermicast. I used to think I could work with pit systems, but now realize that I couldn't deal with getting on my knees, bending over, and reaching down into the pit on a regular basis.  
Stacked Bins
Some worm farmers maximize vertical space by stacking bins on pallet racking (steel shelves that can hold a lot of weight). When the city of Middleton, Connecticut, had a vermicomposting program, they purchased several 8-by-4- foot (2.4 x 1.2 m) worm bins and stacked them on separate pallet rack shelves. Mass Wiggle in Petaluma, California, stacks their warehouse. In total, they have 24 bins that are 70 feet long by 8 feet wide and 22 inches deep (21.3 x 2.4 x 0.6 m).harvested vermicast drops onto the bins below, which are covered with erosion cloth. The workers climb ladders to access the bins at higher levels. 
Continuous Flow-Through Bins
Continuous flow-through (CFT) bins are a type of raised bed system that is very popular for worm farming operations of any size. The open raised beds (built on legs) have a mesh bottom, also called a grate; this design provides air to both the top and bottom of the bin. With this system, you feed the top and harvest vermicast about 2 months later through the bottom. The Manufacturers of CFT systems are few, and many folks have found it more cost-effective to construct their own system, or pay a fabricator or welder to do the job. 
After the bin is set up and ready to go, most people will apply two to four layers of newspaper or one sheet of brown packaging paper on the grate. Then they add 6 inches (15 CM) of moist bedding and introduce worms at a rate of about 1 pound (0.5 kg) per square foot (0.09 m^2) of surface area of the bed space (length multiplied by width, not depth). For example, a CFT bin with a bed measuring 8 feet (2.4 m) long by 5 feet (1.5 m) wide would have 40 square feet (3.7 m^2) of bed space. 
To harvest vermicast off the bottom of a CFT bin, some type of manual or automated mechanism needs to be installed.Many worm farmers install a drive system wit a cable that travels back and forth on top of the grate at the bottom of the bed that slices off one inch (2.5 cm) of castings.


Applying the Pulsing Hydroperiod


An additional definitional issue has to do with the WormWise System is what we have coined as the pulsing hydroperiod. Traditional vermicompost systems will warn you of the dangers of a too moist worm bin and how the anaerobic state will kill the worms and produce toxic, plant killing fertilizer.Usually this is true due to the closed system nature of traditional bins. WW by comparison, utilizing  regular hydration periods, creates a wet system without allowing the anaerobic zone to form from sitting water. The water soluble worm castings are allowed to move freely through the soil matrix ,carrying bioavailable nutrients and microbes to an aeration step termed, the vortex brewer. 
A wetter bin aids in mobility for the worms to move freely through the soil strata to consume waste and breed at an increased rate.Not only the worms are more productive, decomposition rates increase as moisture content increases (Sierra et al, 2017)  only adding to the rapid release of usable nutrients in the worm feed.   Depending on bin size and fertilizer need, up to 60 gallons of worm tea can be harvested a day. And when in a drought, or a lesser fertilizer needed,can be recycled back into the bin for increased potency.

Salinity


Recent research has focused on identifying strategies to improve soil salt levels by utilizing vermicomposting in high-moisture and high-salinity conditions. One such strategy is the use of vermiwash, a liquid fertilizer produced from the excess moisture and organic matter in vermicompost bins. Vermiwash contains high levels of nutrients, growth-promoting bioavailability, and beneficial microorganisms that can improve plant growth and soil health.


Studies have shown that the application of vermiwash can enhance plant growth and increase crop yields in various agricultural settings. Symptoms of saline stress decrease with vermiwash (Ruiz-Lau, 2020). In addition, vermiwash has been found to improve soil structure, increase water-holding capacity, and reduce soil salinity. Furthermore, the use of vermiwash can help to reduce the negative impacts of high moisture and salinity on earthworm health and vermicompost quality.


        Vermicomposting can be challenging when the moisture content and salinity of the waste materials are high and the water source has a high salt concentration (chlorine), as these conditions can negatively impact worm health and reduce the quality of the vermicompost produced. Vermicompost systems with a pulsing hydroperiod generally have lowered salinity due to increased inputs of freshwater. The increased freshwater input of the hydroperiod also increases the worm's tolerance for salt by increasing the threshold to balance the osmotic pressure between the body and the environment (Zexuan et al. 2019)


Overall, vermicomposting can be a valuable tool for organic waste management and sustainable agriculture, and the use of vermiwash can help to overcome some of the challenges associated with high-moisture and high-salinity conditions. Proper salinity (<0.2%) can improve the appetite of earthworms; ( Zexuan et al. 2019) high salinity (measured >0.2%) prevented earthworms from properly growing and had negative effects on quality of products in composting. Further research is needed to optimize the production and application of vermiwash and to understand its potential benefits for soil health and plant growth in different agricultural contexts.


 In any case, soil amendment with vermicast generally results in virtually no return of salt deposits into the soil and humic vermicast is often prescribed for salt saturated soils, as recorded in several different locations:




Redox and Sulfide


Redox is a chemical reaction in which electrons are transferred between molecules, and it plays a significant role in the decomposition of organic matter in vermicompost bins. Redox conditions are affected by several factors, including oxygen availability, microbial activity, and the presence of organic matter. In vermicompost bins, redox conditions are typically aerobic, as the high levels of organic matter, pore space and earthworm activity create an oxygen-rich environment. Aerobic  conditions promote the growth of aerobic bacteria. The heavy moisture periods encourage conditions that are inundated to promote some  anaerobic microbes which play a critical role in the decomposition of organic matter and the production of vermicompost.


Healthy vermi-systems are characterized by a redox system dominated by the oxygen-H2O pair. Because of abundant inputs of organic matter and increased exchange of oxygen into the waterlogged sediments, electron acceptors such as oxygen nitrate, iron (III), and manganese (IV) are readily available. This results in oxidative conditions well beyond the usual zone of aeration and leads to the production of Sulfate SO4(-2) through the oxidation of the elemental sulfur that is present in anaerobic conditions in vermicast systems. 


Sulfide (S-2) is another important factor in the biogeochemistry of vermicompost bins. Sulfide is a byproduct of anaerobic respiration and can accumulate in composting systems, causing negative impacts on plant growth and microbial activity. High levels of sulfide can be toxic to earthworms and other beneficial organisms, reducing the quality of the vermicompost produced.


        For freely drained systems, one of the most significant effects of a frequent hydroperiod is the absence of these reducing, anaerobic conditions. Instead these Vermicompost systems generally have oxygen penetration and high redox potential down to a depth of 60 centimeters [24 inches] or more. One would expect that flooding and restricting flow of these systems would lead to a decrease in redox potential and renewed production of toxic sulfide. 


        Restricted-waterlogged vermicast systems, in contrast, can have low redox potential (due to waterlogging), although they tend  to have little sulfide reduction processes because of the limited oxygen inputs. Reflooding these systems might be expected to lead to relatively little change in redox potential but higher levels of sulfide. 


        Unfortunately, redox and sulfide are not routinely reported in soil restoration programs in the Northeast. Thus, the primary published source of data on these parameters is a greenhouse microcosm study by Portnoy and Giblin (1997b), in which they simulated restoration by flooding sediment cores (from both restricted-drained sites and restricted waterlogged sites) with seawater. They found that flooding with seawater led to decreases in redox potential (Eh) for both the restricted-drained and the restricted-waterlogged sediments, though the decreases were most dramatic for the restricted-drained peat and for the surface layers, where higher amounts of organic matter led to more rapid depletion of electron acceptors.Patterns of sulfide production in these greenhouse experiments are complex. Sulfate reduction was probably taking place post flooding in both restricted-drained and restricted-waterlogged peats, but measured pore water sulfide concentrations were quite low in the restricted-drained peats, apparently because an abundance of iron (II) was available to precipitate the sulfide as iron sulfides. In Contrast, dissolved sulfide levels in the restricted-waterlogged peats rose to levels comparable to a natural marsh (approximately 5 millimolar)


        Measurements of redox potential at managed realignment sites generally corroborate the greenhouse experiments. At both Orplands Farm (MacLeod et al. 1999) and Pillmouth (Blackwell et al. 2004), Eh dropped significantly within a year of flooding, though it continued to drop further in the second year of post flood monitoring at the Orplands Farm.


Recent studies have investigated strategies for managing redox and sulfide levels in vermicompost bins. One approach is the addition of oxygen to the composting system, which can promote aerobic conditions and reduce sulfide accumulation. Our addition comes in the form of the storage and pump system dubbed the vortex brewer (more on this later). Other strategies include the use of amendments such as biochar or activated carbon, which can help to absorb sulfide and improve compost quality.


        There is widespread concern in the vermiculture literature over the potential development of highly anoxic conditions in the vermi-system. That is, as the old vegetation is killed by restriction and buried by rapid sediment deposition (to be discussed), there is the potential for forming a highly organic layer of decomposing material, which, due to the pulsing hydroperiod, may be reducing enough (and at a significant enough speed) to prevent anoxic conditions in the worm system


pH
Soil pH is a critical factor in plant growth and health, as it affects the availability of nutrients and the activity of microorganisms in the soil. Vermicompost has been shown to have a positive effect on soil pH, as it contains high levels of calcium carbonate and other alkaline compounds that can neutralize soil acidity. Furthermore, vermicompost contains beneficial microorganisms that can promote the growth of soil bacteria and fungi, which can further contribute to soil pH regulation.


Vermicompost systems can experience acidic conditions due to the oxidation of reduced sulfur that occurs when air enters the previously anoxic zones (Soukup and Portney 1986). As well as the presence of citric acid from organic matter deposition.  One would expect that the pulsing hydroperiod that mimics the tidal flow  would lead to saturation of the peat, the reestablishment of aerobic soil conditions, the cessation of sulfide reduction, and a return to the circumneutral pH typical of healthy worm clew. 
As was the case for redox and sulfide, there are few data available from North America on pH changes following vermicast soil restoration. In their greenhouse study, Portney and Giblin (1997b) found that pore water pH and alkalinity in the restricted drained peat rose dramatically within 3 months of flooding (from approximately pH 4 to pH 6.5), while the restricted- waterlogged peat stayed near its initial pH of approximately 6.7, though with short term dip to below 6. 
Changes in pH have been reported following managed realignment in the United Kingdom. At a site with a pre-flooding pH of 5.9, MacLeod et al. (1999) observed an increase to 6.5 to 6.9 after flooding without drainage. More surprisingly, Blackwell et al, (2004) observed a sharp, but temporary decrease in pore water pH (10 centimeter depth) following flooding without drainage, with pH values below 5 for up to nine weeks. This pattern was not found in deeper pore water (40 centimeters) or in shallower sediment pH (2 centimeters). The authors speculate that this acidification may result from patchy sulfide oxidation or from organic acids (or carbonic acid) produced by decomposition following OM deposition. 


Studies have shown that the addition of vermicompost to soil can increase soil pH and promote the growth of crops such as tomatoes, peppers, and lettuce. Additionally, vermicompost has been found to improve soil structure and water-holding capacity, which can further enhance plant growth and nutrient uptake.


However, the effect of vermicompost on soil pH can vary depending on the initial pH of the soil and the type of organic material being composted. In some cases, the addition of vermicompost to alkaline soils can lead to an increase in pH, which can negatively affect plant growth. Therefore, it is essential to monitor soil pH levels and adjust the application of vermicompost accordingly.


In conclusion, the biogeochemistry of vermicompost bins is an important area of research that can provide valuable insights into the use of vermicompost for soil health and fertility. The impact of vermicomposting on soil pH is a critical factor that should be considered when using vermicompost in agriculture. Further research is needed to optimize the use of vermicompost for soil pH regulation and to identify the best practices for vermicomposting in different soil types and agricultural settings.




Sediment Deposition and Vertical Accretion


Sediment deposition and vertical accretion are important factors in soil formation and stability, as they contribute to the buildup of soil and the development of soil structure. Studies have shown that the addition of vermicompost to soil can increase sediment deposition and vertical accretion, as the organic material in vermicompost can bind with soil particles and increase their stability.


Healthy marshland soils accrete vertically at roughly the rate of relative sea level rise (RSLR) by accumulating organic matter (Primarily from in situ plant production) and inorganic sediment (from tidal deposition). In describing the rate of this process, three different types of measurements should be distinguished: “accumulation” refers to the rate of mass deposition at or near the surface (measured as the mass of sediment accumulating above a near-surface marker horizon, grams per square centimeter per year); Accretion refers to the rate of vertical growth at or near the surface (measured as the height of sediment accumulating above a marker horizon, millimeters per year); and “elevation change” also includes processes below the marker horizon such as subsidence or swelling (measured by repeated surveying or with a sediment elevation table [SET; Cahoon et al. 2002], millimeters per year).
Tidal restriction severely disrupts natural elevation processes. soil drainage can lead to rapid oxidation, loss of organic matter, and resulting subsidence (Portnoy and Giblin 1997a;Turner 2004). After the initial loss in elevation, some restricted-drained soils may reach a new equilibrium in which relative water levels are somewhat higher and organic and inorganic material begins to accumulate again (Anisfeld et al. 1999). In restricted water-logged soils, organic matter oxidation and subsidence may be less than in restricted- drained soils, but inputs of inorganic material may still be severely reduced (Portnoy and Giblin 1997a).
There is ample evidence from a variety of locations that tidal restoration can lead to rapid accumulation, accretion, and elevation change, especially in the short term, as the soil “catches up” to the higher water level.  


Does the weight of this evidence mea that restoration sites can always adjust to the higher water level? Of course not. Depending on the degree of subsidence and the amount of RSLR that occurred during the period of restriction, tidal restoration may result in at least temporary drowning of the soil, in which the system is converted to unvegetated intertidal mudflat or subtidal open water. This happened, for example,at Great Harbor Marsh/Lost Lake, Connecticut, where an unintentional tidal restoration resulted in conversion of restricted marsh to open water (Rozsa 1995); One part of this system slowly regained elevation and became marsh, but another portion remains open water. An additional risk of full restoration is the flooding of nearby structures that are built under the assumption that the lower water levels provided by the restriction would endure forever (Roman et al. 1995). If one wants to avoid these risks, it is important to carefully design restoration projects to produce flooding levels that are high enough to lead to desired changes ( e.g. restored soil functions, control of invasive plants), but not so high that nearby houses are flooded or the tolerance of low marsh vegetation such as spartina Alterniflora (smooth cordgrass) is exceeded. 
However under certain circumstances, it can work well to “aim low”; that is, design a relatively  wet system  that may be unvegetated for several years  but will eventually undergo renewed soil development: sediment accretion, leaching to a reduced hydroperiod, leading to initial colonization by vegetation, leading to further sediment trapping and organic matter accumulation, and so forth. Thus has been the strategy of the San Francisco bAY (Williams and Orr 2002) and to a lesser extent, at several sites in Connecticut (P. Capotosto, pers. comm) This approach should be taken only where three conditions apply: there are no nearby structures that would be flooded (or any such structures can be brought out); there is a willingness to accept several years of unvegetated conditionals ; and hydrologic and sedimentological circumstances are favorable for sediment trapping.
        Regarding the last of these conditions, Williams and Orr (2002) identify three factors that they consider to be the most important in determining whether sedimentation and soil development are likely at breached-levee sites: the availability of suspended sediment, the presence or absence of wind waves that can lead to sediment erosion;and the extent to which tidal exchange is still restricted in a way that can limit sediment delivery. It is likely that at most formerly restricted sites, the hydrology and sedimentology do tend to favor sedimentation, simply because these are generally sheltered sites that at some point did experience sufficient sedimentation for soil development. 
        However, current conditions at some sites may be significantly different from the conditions under which the soil initially developed: RSLR may have increased, sediment availability may have decreased, and other factors may be interfering with the ability of soils to keep up with RSLR. In the face of predicted accelerations in RSLR (IPCC 2007) and documented- but still not fully understood- cases of marsh drowning (e.g.,Hartig et al. 2002), it seems prudent to require careful planning and monitoring in the creation of wetter soils in the twenty-first century. A tidally restricted Phragmites- dominated marsh may be a wiser choice than a “restored” drowned system that is too wet to support marsh vegetation, especially as more and more soils are lost to drowning. The restoration imperative and the preservation imperative are coming into conflict, and determining how to maximize the quantity and quality of tidal marsh will require creative thinking. 


However, the impact of vermicomposting on sediment deposition and vertical accretion can vary depending on the type and quality of organic material being composted, as well as the initial soil conditions. In some cases, the addition of vermicompost to soil can lead to excessive sediment deposition and vertical accretion, which can negatively affect soil structure and plant growth.


                There is also some concern over potential subsidence associated with restoration at waterlogged sites. In their greenhouse experiment, Portnoy and Giblin (1997b) found that flooding of the restricted water-logged soils with seawater led to significant subsidence of 60 to 80 millimeters over a short time period of five months. This was apparently due to the combination of salinity-induced plant mortality and sulfate induced accelerated decomposition. This is potentially of concern for the viability of tidal restoration of restricted-waterlogged sites, since this amount of subsidence could lead to conditions too wet for halophytic vegetation to establish itself. However, it is important to note that the greenhouse experiment did not include tidal delivery of sediment. As shown earlier, this is a huge factor in the elevation dynamics of restored soils, especially in the short term, and may well mitigate any subsidence. There have been no field reports of subsidence in the period following tidal restoration.
One approach for dealing with excessive flooding in tidal restoration projects is to artificially increase the marsh elevation through the placement of fill material. Cornu and Sadro (2002) evaluated the success of this approach in four marshes in Coos Bay, Oregon, which had received different amounts of fill material as part of a tidal restoration. They found that all sites experienced significant shallow subsidence and elevation loss, apparently due to compaction of the ill material. Subsidence was greatest at the site with the greatest amount of fill. soil restoration projects need to take into account the possibility for compaction when designing soil elevations using fill material. 


Furthermore, the presence of earthworms in vermicompost bins can enhance sediment deposition and vertical accretion, as earthworms can create soil channels and burrows that allow for better water infiltration and soil aeration. This, in turn, can promote the growth of beneficial microorganisms and improve soil structure and fertility.


In conclusion, the biogeochemistry of vermicompost bins is an important area of research that can provide valuable insights into the use of vermicompost for soil health and fertility. The impact of vermicomposting on sediment deposition and vertical accretion is a critical factor that should be considered when using vermicompost in agriculture. Further research is needed to optimize the use of vermicompost for sediment deposition and vertical accretion and to identify the best practices for vermicomposting in different soil types and agricultural settings.




Carbon, Nitrogen, and Phosphorus


Carbon, nitrogen, and phosphorus are essential nutrients for plant growth, and the availability of these nutrients in the soil can impact crop productivity. Vermicompost has been shown to increase the levels of these nutrients in soil, as earthworms and microorganisms present in the vermicompost help to break down organic matter and release these nutrients into the soil.
Sequestration of carbon ©, nitrogen (N), and phosphorus (P)in peat is an important function provided by healthy soils, and restoring that function can be one of the goals of soil restoration (Andrews et al. 2006, 2008). As already discussed, drained soils experience oxidation which leads to decomposition of organic matter and reduced sediment C concentrations (Portnoy and Giblin 1997a; Portnoy 1999). Anisfeld et al. (1999) found that marshes that had been restricted more than ninety years showed evidence of having experienced an oxidation phase, resulting in low C levels at depth, but had reached a new equilibrium in which surface C concentrations were not significantly lower than those of reference sites. Even during the oxidation phase, much of the sediment N and P appears to be retained in the sediment (Portnoy and Giblin 1997a).
However, the impact of vermicomposting on the cycling of carbon, nitrogen, and phosphorus can vary depending on the type and quality of organic material being composted. For example, the carbon-to-nitrogen ratio of the organic material can impact the rate of decomposition and the release of nutrients into the soil.
        Restoration might be expected to lead to increased C accumulation, as higher water levels reduce decomposition rates. Evidence from the accretion studies cited earlier tends to suggest, however, that the primary source of new material is inorganic sedimentation and that increases in C accumulation are more minor. Likewise, Anisfeld et al. (1999) found that restored marshes did not have significantly higher sediment organic matter concentrations (percentage) or rates of C accumulation (grams per square centimeter per year) than restricted sites. N accumulation rates were slightly higher in these restored soils than in the restricted and reference sites. 


In contrast, studies in constructed marshes- with their low initial C concentrations - have generally found that sediment C content increased gradually over time as organic matter accumulated due to high productivity and low decomposition rates. In constructed sites in Great Bay Estuary (Morgan and Short 2002), soil organic matter took fifteen to twenty years to reach reference levels, while in California (Zedler and Callaway 1999), soil organic matter appeared to level off after six years, but at a concentration that was still approximately 25 percent lower than that of reference sites. The California study also measured sediment N , which appeared to increase steadily over time but reached only approximately 60 percent of reference at the end of the study period (eleven years)
Craft et al. (1999, 2003( have carried out extensive studies of C, N, and P dynamics in constructed marshes of different ages, each paired with a nearby reference marsh that is similar in many of the controlling variables. They found that concentrations of C and N tended to increase with time since marsh construction, but that even twenty-eight year old marshes were significantly lower in C and N than their reference sites. P behaved differently : levels did not increase over time and constructed marshes are often higher than reference sites , probably due to increased P sorption capacity in the more inorganic soils. Craft et al. (2003) also found that the organic matter in constructed marsh sediments was generally more labile than in reference Marshes (presumably because it is younger), and that C mineralization rates per unit of sediment organic matter were higher in constructed marshes. Despite this, overall C accumulation rates were similar in the constructed and reference marshes,even in the youngest systems.P accumulation rates were also similar across systems, while N accumulation rates were slightly  higher in the constructed soils. 
In the short term (weeks to months) after restoration, there is the possibility of loss of stored nutrients from the sediment due to ion exchange with sea water, Portnoy and Giblin (1997b) found that flooding led to shard increases in pore water ammonium (NH4+) and, to a lesser extent, phosphate (PO4-3) in their greenhouse experiments. Blackwell et al. (2004) found a similarly dramatic increase in pore water NH4+ at the Pillmouth managed realignment project: levels remained elevated (up to 15 mg NH4+-N/L) for several months. 
Furthermore, vermicompost has a higher nitrogen content compared to other types of compost due to the presence of earthworm excrement, which is rich in nitrogen. This can help to promote plant growth and increase crop yields.
In conclusion, the biogeochemistry of vermicompost bins is an important area of research that can provide valuable insights into the use of vermicompost for soil health and fertility. The impact of vermicomposting on the cycling of carbon, nitrogen, and phosphorus is a critical factor that should be considered when using vermicompost in agriculture. Further research is needed to optimize the use of vermicompost for nutrient cycling and to identify the best practices for vermicomposting in different soil types and agricultural settings.


Bulk Density


Bulk density is an important parameter that reflects the compaction and porosity of soil, which can affect plant growth and crop yields. Recent research has investigated the impact of vermicompost on the bulk density of soil and the biogeochemistry of vermicompost bins.        


Healthy soils generally exhibit low bulk densities. Drainage tends to cause collapse of pore space, loss of organic matter, and an increase in density (Portnoy and Giblin 1997a),  although some restricted soils may recover and reach a new equilibrium characterized by relatively low bulk densities (Anisfeld et al. 1999). Agricultural use of the drained marsh (common in Europe) can lead to significant compaction (French 2006). 
For restricted soils that do have high bulk densities, restoration of tidal flow should lead to a decrease in bulk density through two processes: swelling of the marsh as it fills with water (Paquette et al. 2004); and an increase in organic matter and associated porosity. The former should be quite fast, while the latter may take decades, as already discussed. 
Studies have shown that the addition of vermicompost to soil can decrease bulk density, as the organic matter in vermicompost can help to improve soil structure and porosity. The presence of earthworms in vermicompost bins can further enhance this effect, as earthworms can create soil channels and burrows that allow for better water infiltration and soil aeration.
        Spencer et al. (2008) have shown that, at managed realignment sites, the relict agricultural surface may persist as an identifiable layer in the soil for a long time.  Below that level, density is high and moisture content is low. This may significantly impede water and nutrient movement within the soil, and could potentially affect plant health and soil development (Spencer et al. 2008; French 2006). 


In conclusion, the biogeochemistry of vermicompost bins is an important area of research that can provide valuable insights into the use of vermicompost for soil health and fertility. The impact of vermicomposting on bulk density is a critical factor that should be considered when using vermicompost in agriculture. Further research is needed to optimize the use of vermicompost for soil structure and porosity, and to identify the best practices for vermicomposting in different soil types and agricultural settings. Moreover, vermicompost can improve soil organic matter content, which can have long-term benefits for soil fertility and plant growth. This is because soil organic matter can help to stabilize soil aggregates, increase soil water-holding capacity, and enhance nutrient availability.


Metals


        Soil metals are a critical component of soil health and can impact plant growth and human health. Recent research has investigated the impact of vermicomposting on the biogeochemistry of soil metals in vermicompost bins. Healthy bioactive soils are considered sinks for trace metals, which are bound tightly as organic complexes or as sulfides. Tidal restriction can lead to oxidation of sulfides and organics and result in the solubilization of metals into pore water. Exchange between pore water and surface water then lead to these regions becoming a source of previously immobilized metals. Acidification can accelerate the process by increasing metal solubility (Anisfeld and Ben oil 1997). 
Studies have shown that the addition of vermicompost to soil can improve soil metal availability and reduce metal toxicity. This is because earthworms in vermicompost bins can help to transform soil metals into less toxic forms, such as metal sulfides, which are less bioavailable to plants and animals. Furthermore, the organic matter in vermicompost can chelate soil metals, which can increase their availability to plants.
        Soil restoration is expected to reverse this process and lead to renewed metal sequestration, especially since the restored soil is often rapidly accumulating sediment (see earlier discussion). Surprisingly, there have been few, if any, measurements of metal accumulation rates following restoration. However, Andrews et al. (2008) measured metal accumulation rates at a natural soil that they consider to be an analogue for managed realignment sites. They estimated that restoration of flow to 26 square kilometers (10 Square miles) of land in the Humber Estuary (UK) could lead to removal of significant quantities of metals (e.g., 6 tons per year of zinc, 3 tons per year of lead). Andrews et al. (2006) used similar methods to estimate metal removal rates for the Humber Estuary under an “extended Deep Green” scenario, in which tides are restored to approximately 85 square kilometers (33 square miles) of the estuary. They found that Arsenic (As) removal would amount to approximately 85 percent of particulate As inputs to the estuary, while copper and lead removal would be approximately 9 percent of particulate inputs. They calculate the economic benefit of copper removal as approximately 1600 dollars per year.






        However, the impact of vermicomposting on soil metal concentrations can vary depending on the type and quality of organic material being composted, as well as the initial soil metal concentrations. In some cases, vermicomposting can lead to the accumulation of soil metals in vermicompost, which can be problematic if the vermicompost is used in areas where soil metal concentrations are already high. In their greenhouse experiments, portnoy and Giblin (1997b) found that flooding of the restricted- drained sediment led to release of iron (II) and aluminum, leading to high concentrations in pore water. They attribute this to a combination of cation exchange and chemical reduction/solubilization. 
        Another element that might be susceptible to mobilization during restoration is cadmium, due to its relatively higher solubility (as chloride complexes) in salt water compared to freshwater. At both the Orplands farm (MacLeod et l. 1999) and Tollesbury (Chang et al. 2001) sites, sediment cadmium concentrations were lower after restoration than before, implying some loss of cadmium from marsh sediments to the estuary. At both sites, other metals mostly did not change significantly, though there were some relatively small increases in sediment concentrations of lead (Orplands Fram) and copper (Tollesbury), indicating additional sequestration. 
In conclusion, the biogeochemistry of vermicompost bins is an important area of research that can provide valuable insights into the use of vermicompost for soil health and fertility. The impact of vermicomposting on soil metal concentrations is a critical factor that should be considered when using vermicompost in agriculture. Further research is needed to optimize the use of vermicompost for soil metal availability and to identify the best practices for vermicomposting in different soil types and agricultural settings.


Other Pollutants 


        Soil pollutants, excluding metals, can have a significant impact on soil health and plant growth. Recent research has investigated the impact of vermicomposting on the biogeochemistry of soil pollutants in vermicompost bins. Traditional closed system vermicompost  can lead to poor flushing of processed compost material which- coupled with release of partly decomposed organic matter from oxidizing conditions- can cause water quality issues such as low dissolved oxygen and high levels of bacteria that could indicate human health problems (Soukup and Portnoy 1986; Portnoy 1991; Portnoy and Allen 2006). It is expected that vermicompost would significantly reduce these problems (Portnoy and Allen 2006; Maris et al. 2007), although it is also possible that increased loss of ammonium (NH4+0 and dissolved organic carbon from the soil and nutrient sediments could lead to increased oxygen demand. IN any case, there is little to no documentation in the marsh restoration literature of the response of oxygen (O2) and indicator bacteria in tidal channels.
Restoration should also lead to indirect improvements in hypoxia due to nutrient removal. Marshes are hot spots for denitrification, and the tidal conditions in restored marshes (along with high primary productivity) should favor increased denitrification. Studies have shown that the addition of vermicompost to soil can help to remediate soil pollutants and reduce their toxicity. This is because earthworms in vermicompost bins can help to transform organic pollutants into less toxic forms through a process called biodegradation. Additionally, vermicompost can adsorb or absorb some soil pollutants, leading to their removal or immobilization in the soil.
The impact of vermicomposting on soil pollutant concentrations can vary depending on the type and quality of organic material being composted, as well as the initial pollutant concentrations. In some cases, vermicomposting can lead to the accumulation of soil pollutants in vermicompost, which can be problematic if the vermicompost is used in areas where soil pollutant concentrations are already high.
In conclusion, the biogeochemistry of vermicompost bins is an important area of research that can provide valuable insights into the use of vermicompost for soil health and fertility. The impact of vermicomposting on soil pollutants, excluding metals, is a critical factor that should be considered when using vermicompost in agriculture. Further research is needed to optimize the use of vermicompost for the remediation of soil pollutants and to identify the best practices for vermicomposting in different soil types and agricultural settings.
